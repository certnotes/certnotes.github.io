In many large organisations, technical subject matter experts (SMEs) are quietly essential yet chronically undervalued. They don’t always sit in formal leadership roles, they may not manage large teams, and their work can be hard to translate into tidy KPIs. Beneath the organisational chart, SMEs often function as the living interface between legacy systems, operational constraints, and institutional knowledge that keeps complex environments stable. When that expertise is treated as interchangeable, peripheral, or “just technical”, the organisation develops a particular kind of vulnerability. *Key person risk*.

In organisations that optimise against cost to the detriment of capability and resilience, key person risk is commonly framed as a localised issue. If one person leaves, their tasks can be reassigned. In technical domains, that framing is dangerously incomplete. Many SMEs carry knowledge that is not only rare but non-transferable at speed. They understand why a system behaves a certain way, which undocumented workaround prevents outages, how data actually flows. Documentation helps, but it rarely captures nuance, context, and judgment. Especially not in environments shaped by years of incremental patches, shifting requirements, competing priorities, and under-funding of adjacent technical roles.

When an SME’s unique skillset is absent, the risk doesn’t stay contained. It can spread across the organisation. Small errors compound because teams lose the ability to diagnose root causes. Troubleshooting becomes guesswork. Fixes become overly cautious or overly aggressive. Delivery slows as dependencies are rediscovered the hard way. Controls weaken as no one remembers why they exist. The cost isn’t merely replacement hiring. It’s the erosion of reliability, speed, visibility, and decision quality across functions. In effect, the organisation transitions from “expert-led certainty” to “process-led approximation,” which can be brittle when systems are complex and trigger realisation of latent risk across the organisation.

This vulnerability becomes most exposed during periods sensitive to surfacing of latent risk. 
For example in mergers and demergers, when the organisation needs to separate, integrate, re-platform, or re-map critical capabilities under strict deadlines. These events create discontinuities. Teams are reorganised, incentives shift, and new governance models appear overnight. In that context, SMEs are not just implementers, they are risk dampeners. They help identify hidden coupling between systems, anticipate failure modes, validate assumptions, and mitigate against technical debt turning into operational failures.

If key person risk is realised mid-transaction (through resignation, burnout, or mismanagement), exposure can spiral quickly. Migration plans slip, controls break, data quality degrades, and operational incidents multiply. Each incident increases scrutiny, urgency, and rework. Further draining capacity and making additional failures more likely. What started as one undervalued role becomes a cascading organisational hazard. That penny pinched from not aligning remuneration with key person risk (through ignorance or parsimony) might end up as the thousand dollar punch that breaks the billion dollar (but poorly engineered) dam.

However, if they are unable to identify or treat technology risk in a way that results in you being compelled to consider re-training or changing industries, the interest rate on those few dollars will turn out to be quite high.</p>

Critical SMEs need to be treated as strategic assets through succession planning, knowledge transfer programs, and meaningful career pathways. It is a core risk management requirement, especially for organisations that routinely operate at scale, complexity, and under M&A/separation pressures.
