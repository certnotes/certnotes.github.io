Agentic AI is changing the unit economics of knowledge work. In the past, even the best individual contributors were bounded by human throughput with attention, communication overhead, and the friction of coordination with others. A great employee might reliably produce the output of two or three people. Impressive, but not structurally disruptive. Agentic AI breaks that ceiling by turning one person into the orchestrator of many semi-autonomous “digital workers” that can plan, draft, test, iterate, and report in parallel.

The key shift is that AI assistance widens the spectrum of productivity that is possible. At the low end, it can be a modest boost by automating routine tasks, speeding up writing, summarising meetings. At the high end, an individual with domain expertise, systems thinking, and architectural experience can spin up agents to conduct research, develop products, execute tests, and maintain documentation continuously. That same person becomes an orchestrator of processes rather than a producer of every artefact. In the medium term, it’s plausible that a high performer with robust AI workflows can generate the output of an entire small team, possibly seven to ten people’s worth of output. In the long term, as agent reliability, tool access, and verification improve, it’s likely that a single exceptional operator could coordinate ten to twenty “equivalent heads” of work across tightly scoped domains with strong verification.

As organisations capitalise on this, management structures will flatten. There will likely be meaningfully fewer people to manage across all white-collar professions. The marginal cost, benefit, and risk of an additional AI agent compared to an employee will fundamentally shift labour economics. The productivity shock as agentic AI spreads across the economy means that you can't compete against an AI-native organisation by hiring more people to do work the usual way. The marginal cost of an employee can't compete against the order of magnitude lower marginal cost of an additional AI agent. While organisations will be expected to operate at higher and higher efficiencies, the pool of capable AI orchestrators will not grow enough relative to demand as education systems take time to adapt to producing AI orchestrators at scale in the short and medium term. Both the need for more employees and the supply of AI orchestration capable employees will be constrained. The organisations that slot incapable people into orchestration roles will soon realise that AI is a double edged sword that augments competence as well as failure.

The consequence is that, instead of many managers each overseeing small teams(and managers to oversee those managers), fewer managers will oversee larger teams of high-leverage individuals, each augmented by agent swarms. Middle management roles that exist primarily to translate, relay, or supervise routine execution will be the most vulnerable. However, flattening doesn’t eliminate management, it raises the bar. When a single person can drive the output of multiple teams, the cost of losing that person is loss of organisational function. Key-person risk becomes operational risk. Organisations will need stronger redundancy, knowledge capture, security controls, and succession planning. They’ll need managers who can build resilient teams with standardised workflows, clear interfaces, and adaptable structure. The best managers will be those who can design environments where AI-augmented teams become greater than the sum of their parts through tooling, training, governance, and culture. They will avoid turning the organisation into a fragile collection of lone geniuses.

This is also where “crab-bucket” managers become dangerous. Managers who exploit emotional vulnerabilities and engineer deceit to suppress salaries, block mobility, and extract loyalty may have been tolerated as an unpleasant cost of doing business. In an agentic world, they are instigators of outsized risk. High-caliber people who can orchestrate AI at scale do so from a foundation of cognitive function that isn't limited to "work", they will not and need not accept management compliance techniques. They will just leave. When one departure can remove the output of multiple teams, you can't risk those kind of games, and you can't risk those kind of managers. In the age of agentic AI, respect, autonomy, and execution-focused leadership won’t be cultural niceties. They’ll be core risk controls.
