In many technology organisations, the most capable Individual Contributors (ICs) eventually feel a subtle push toward management. The message is rarely explicit, but it’s familiar. Management is the next level. Leadership means people management. *This is how you grow.* For technically capable ICs, that move can be a costly trade. It often swaps a form of value that is highly verifiable for one that is fundamentally ambiguous and politically fragile.

Technical competence can be assured with relatively high certainty by independent third parties. Degrees, certifications, open-source contributions, shipped systems, and delivered projects all leave artifacts. Even when people disagree about priorities or architecture, the underlying record exists. The code ran, the service scaled, the latency dropped, the system shipped. Your reputation as an IC is anchored to outcomes that can be inspected and validated by people who have never met your manager.

Management competence is different. Much of it is hard to verify. “Good manager” is often a story told through proxies. Retention, morale, alignment, stakeholder satisfaction, and team velocity. Signals that are noisy, lagging, and heavily confounded by budget, staffing, executive priorities, and sheer luck. Two managers can face the same conditions and get wildly different evaluations based on narrative, visibility, and the political climate. Worse, the strongest “evidence” of your managerial skill is frequently your current or previous manager’s endorsement. An endorsement that can be withdrawn, minimised, or reframed with ease.

That creates a one-sided vulnerability. As an IC, your receipts travel with you. As a manager, your credibility can be bought into doubt by someone that's insecure, holds a grudge, or has a competing agenda. No evidence need be supplied. The deciding factor may just be if your reputation can shield you from the plausibility of the accusation. A single critical performance review, a reorg, or a stakeholder conflict can bend your entire career trajectory. When you leave a company, you may discover that your managerial impact was never captured in durable artifacts, only in opinions, and opinions don’t transfer cleanly. Not between companies, not between industries, and not between social networks of professional trust and/or alliance.

As a manager you are only as effective as the utility of political capital you have built up. You trade some horses, and hope that the ones you end up with can take you from point A to point B. Accumulation of political capital can not be automated, and is often tedious, undignified work. You can never be sure that you have ended up with the targeted quality and quantity of political capital. The time and energy that you have spent could be wasted if a reorg sidelines your most effective allies, or if sabotage is covertly and effectively executed.

If you’re a highly capable technologist, consider what you may be giving up. Deep craft, technical intuition, the ability to build and debug under pressure, and a portfolio that compounds over time. Those skills are scarce, marketable, and objectively defensible. Meanwhile, management may offer relatively uncapped earning potential at the top, but often at the cost of being evaluated through ambiguity, and facing risks that are difficult to manage or just unmanageable. There’s an alternative, stay on the technical track and pursue leadership through architecture, mentorship, and ownership of outcomes. If you can lead with code, design, and delivery, why exchange certainty for stories?
