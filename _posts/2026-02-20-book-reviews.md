I suspect that it may be many years, if ever, that I will again have the freedom to engage in freewheeling intellectual inquiry (relatively) unconstrained by the typical strictures of life. So while my freedom lasts, instead of the ethnography of finance corporations I planned to write, on how broke managers made bad managers, how managers in business units with low barriers to entry are little more than corporate bouncers, and how the management track is designed to capture (and trap) promising talent as internal assets (though I might still squeeze the last one out at some point), I think instead I should dedicate my time to pulling a few gold nuggets out of the fibrous stalagmites that I have surrounded myself with.

I still have education and work commitments to attend to, but I think one trade paperback review per week or two shouldn't be too onerous. I am sick of reading about AI, and I am sick of writing about AI, so I think I will focus on the humanities and the social sciences, but still with an eye on what will be interesting and useful for readers. One A4 page at size 12 font is approximately 500 words, that is what I've limited myself to in previous posts and a good practice to continue for book reviews. At an average reading speed (200 to 250 wpm) it's only about 2-3 minutes. One thing I've noticed is that academics write to have written and journalists write to be read, and the latter should be the aim of any writing. The foreword to the Penguin Classics edition of *Meditations* by Marcus Aurelius was such a festering bloated ouroboros of navel-gazing prose that I have always kept in mind to never write like that. Likewise, never be like the academic that writes textbooks to have the most technically correct explanations entombed in discipline-specific jargon to avoid criticism from colleagues but leave students who actually buy the books perplexed.

I bought a copy of *Collins Complete Writing Guide* a couple of years ago, and I think I should try to finish it before AI completely demolish what little remains of the writing professions. Though I wonder if I am doing the modern equivalent of learning how to write in cursive or calculate with an abacus. A book review on a writing guide has narrower appeal than most, so I think I will skip it as a subject of review and just read it alongside the books I will review.

I am halfway through Paul Bloom's *Psych: The Story of the Human Mind* and that would be a good first book to review here. The allure of online content and interaction has always been that they are an accessible facsimile of reality. The rise of AI is rendering that untrue and will only increase in untruth as time goes on. Skills of in-person interactions with individuals and social systems will be at a premium and it will be strategic to build on that when possible. I think that is a good topic to focus on for this series of book reviews, books that build valuable in-person skills for environments where there is and will always have a clear absence of AI assistance. On a side note, AI processing requires at a bare minimum some kind of structuring of input data, most often encoding statements or queries as prose. You can still throw a ball of word-yarn at AI the way Joyce wrote *Ulysses*, but a ball of word-yarn is what you'll get back, so you better know how to knit. *Collins Complete Writing Guide* might actually be generally useful for future careers where AI prompting is a non-optional skill, so I will write a review of it when I am done.

Genuine writing at its core is an act of meditation and self-reflection, a snapshot of a mind looking at itself in the mirror. I encourage everyone to write a little, at least once a week, even if it's just in a diary that no one else will read that you set on fire at the end of the year in a ritual of regenerative self-immolation (I saw this on a TV show many years ago and has stuck out in my mind ever since).
